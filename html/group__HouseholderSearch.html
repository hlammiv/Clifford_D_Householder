For a given f value, this function fills the std::vectors x1_cands, x2_cands, and lookup with all potential candidates for the entries \( x_1, x_2, \) and \( x_3 \) respectively, subject to the \( epsilon \)-condition.

Analogously to the results of the Kalra et. al. paper, any choice of \( x_1, x_2, \) and \( x_3 \) must satisfy

\[ q(x_1) + q(x_2) + q(x_3) = 2\cdot 3^{2f}. \]

Since \( q(x) \) is positive-definite on the coefficients (the entries of the array 'element'), we may loop over all possibilities \( x \), which necessarily satisfy \( q(x) \leq 2 \cdot 3^{2f} \).

For each \( x \) looped over, we check whether \( x \) is within \( \epsilon/(2c\sqrt{2}) \) of the three values: \( e^{i \theta/2}, -1, 0 \). Accordingly, \( x \) is placed into x1_cands, x2_cands, and/or x3_cands respectively.

After looping through all possibilities, the entries of each of the 3 vectors are sorted by how close they are to their respective target values.

Performs Householder reflector search to approximate \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \) up to \( \epsilon \) by finding a vector \( u \in \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}]^3 \) close to \( [e^{i\theta/2}, -1, 0] \).

We are trying to find a vector \( u := 3^{-f}[ x_1, x_2, x_3 ]^T \) with entries in \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}] \) differing from the vector \( [e^{i\theta/2}, -1, 0] \) in the Frobenius norm by at most \( \epsilon/(2c\sqrt{2}) \). We wish to choose the entries of \( u \) so that \( \| u \|^2 = 2 \). The Householder reflector is then obtained by taking \( H : = I_3 - u^T u \). Once this \( H \) is found, we have that \( X_{(0,1)} H \) differs from the target unitary matrix \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \) by \( \epsilon \) in the Frobenius norm.

Following along with the paper by Kalra et. al., we have a positive definite function \( q: \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9] \to \mathbb{Z} \), where \( x/3^f \) lies in a 3-vector of norm \( \sqrt{2} \) only if \( q(x) \leq 2\cdot 3^{2f} \). Since \( q \) is a positive-definite function on the coefficients of the coefficients (stored in the array 'element'), we are able to loop over all such \( x \) for a given \( f \). This is done in the function entryEnumeration, where all candidates of \( x_i (i=1,2,3) \) are found in one function call.

Once all candidates are found for each of the three entries, we loop over all possible combinations of \( x_1 \) and \( x_2 \). With a given choice of the first two entries, the third entry must satisfy

\[ |x_3|^2 = 2\cdot 3^{2f} - |x_1|^2 - |x_2|^2. \]

Solving equations of the form \( |x|^2 = a \) for \( x \) appears to be difficult or this ring and seems to require us to solve a system of 6 quadratic forms in 18 variables. I do not believe that there is an efficient way to do this. This is why we collect a list of \( x_3 \) candidates in the std::vector 'lookup.' For each element of this std::vector we test whether the given \( x_3 \) satisfies the above equation.

If a valid \( x_3 \) is found that completes the vector, the \( epsilon \)-condition is tested. If passed, the three entries are passed as a std::array. Otherwise, the next \( x_3 \) is tested. If all possible combinations of \( x_1, x_2, \) and \( x_3 \) are exhausted, the value of f is incremented, and we start again.

Parameters
thetaThe angle of the desired two-level Givens rotator \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \).
epsilonDesired level of precision of unitary to \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \).
max_fLargest value of f to be tested. Prevents program from running for an arbitrarily long time.
Returns
A std::array 'u' of 3 elements in \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}] \), where \( X_{(0,1)}(I_3 - u^T u) \) approximates \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \) within \( \epsilon \).
