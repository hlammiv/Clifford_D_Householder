Detailed Description

This file contains ESA (exhaustive search algorithm) and all of the component functions it calls. The algorithm is a modification of the exhaustive search algorithm described here, where we instead work over the Clifford \( + D \) froup as opposed to the Clifford \( + R \) group. Specifically, the algorithm tries to approximate the matrix \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \) up to a precision of \( \epsilon \) under the Frobenius norm with an element of the unitary group \( U(\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}]) \). Here, \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) = \mbox{Diag}(e^{-i\theta/2}, e^{i\theta/2},1 \). The algorithm follows the same general process outlined in Algorithm 1 of the original paper. Our analog of the ENUMERATECANDIDATES routine is the fullDiagEnumeration, and for COMPLETEUNITARY, exhaustiveCompleteUnitary.

That this algorithm is truly exhaustive relies primarily on Theorem 4.3 here. In particular, they work with the ring \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, (1 - \zeta)^{-1}] \). This ring is really the same as \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}] \). To see this, note that \((1 - \zeta_9)^9 \) is divisible by 3 when expressed in the natural \( \mathbb{Z} \)-basis \( \{ \zeta_9^i \}_{i=0}^5 \). Thus

\[ 1/3 = \frac{(1 - \zeta_9)^9 / 3}{(1-\zeta_9)^9} \in \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, (1 - \zeta_9)^{-1}]. \]

Likewise, taking the derivative on both sides of the formula for a finite geometric squence, one can obtain

\[ (1 - \zeta_9)^{-1} = \frac{-1}{3^2} \sum_{i=1}^{8} ix^i \in \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}] \]

Knowing that these two rings are the same, we will refer to this ring as \( R_D \). Now, \( R_D \) is dense in the complex plane, which poses a challenge enumerating ALL candidates for a diagonal element. However, Theorem 4.3 mentioned above states that there are finitely many elements of \( R_D \) which arise as entries of a unit vector for a given \( f \). (Note that replacing their \( \chi^f \) with \( 3^f \) makes no fundamental difference except for the specific values of \( A, B, \) and \( C \) in their quadratic forms.) The relations given in the theorem also provide us with a positive definite quadratic form \( q \) with an upper bound given in terms of \( f \). This allows us to obtain ALL diagonal candidates by means of a nested for-loop (see fullDiagEnumeration), and we can also form a lookup table for candidates belowt the main diagonal. Once a main diagonal and the lower entries determined, the entries above the diagonal can be found algebraically (see fillUpper).

Note
We store our diagonal candidates and lookup table in a std::forward_list. I prefer these to std::vectors since vectors are stored in one contiguous chunk of memory, whereas forward_lists do not require this. Initially, I implemented this with vectors, but this seems as though it could lead to memory issues as f is increased and the lookup table gets larger. A downside is that although we no longer require large contiguous chunks of memory, we ultimately use more memory as each element of the list also has a pointer to the next element. Also, as forward_lists are somewhat more primitive, we can only iterate through these in one direction, and we cannot directly compute the size.

Performs a exhaustive search to approximate \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \) with an element of \( \mbox{U}(3, \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}]) \).

Our code somewhat closely resembles the pseudocode for the ESA (Algorithm 1) in the original paper. However, since we take our denominators to be powers of 3 instead of \( \chi = 1 + 2\zeta_3 \), there is no change-of-variable as was done in line 2 of Algorithm 1. The algorithm starts with f = 0, but if no satisfactory unitary is found, f is incremented, and the search process repeats. After the algorithm terminates will have a \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix of the form

\[ 3^{-f} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & y_1 & z_1 \\ x_2 & y_2 & z_2 \\ z_3 & y_3 & z_3 \end{bmatrix} \]

where the main diagonal and lower entries will be elements of \( \mathbb{Z}[zeta_9] \).

Parameters
thetaThe angle of the desired two-level Givens rotator \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \).
epsilonDesired level of precision.
max_fLargest value of f to be tested. Prevents Program from running for an arbitrarily long time.
Returns
A \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix \( V \in \mbox{U}(3, \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9, \frac{1}{3}]) \) such that \( \| R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) - V \| < \epsilon \) under the Frobenius norm. This matrix \( V \) is stored as a std::array with the first three entries corresponding to the first column, the next three entries corresponding to the second column, etc.

Performs ESA, but sorts diagonal and x_3 candidates by \( \mbox{sde}_\chi \) for faster runtime.

Lemma 5.1 in the Kalra paper states that the entries in our matrices must all have the same sde in terms of chi. Since fullDiagEnumeration and fullX3Enumeration discarded all candidates with \( \mbox{sde}_\chi > 5\), (equivalent to divisibility by 3), we sort our candidates by their sde's in terms of chi, and try to complete the unitary ONLY with candidates whose sde matches. This provides a speedup.

Given a choice of diagonal candidates, this routine attempts to complete the remaining entries of the unitary.

The algorithm loops through lookup looking for \( x_3 \) candidates. For a given candidate, lookup is looped through once again looking for candidates of \( x_2 \) and \( y_3 \) which make the first column and third row unit vectors. If and when valid candidates are found, the routine calls fillUpper which tries to fill entries aove the main diagonal. If this succeeds, the matrix is checked against the epsilon condition and to see whether the matrix is unitary. If no choice for \( x_3 \) in lookup is suitable, the zero matrix is returned.

Fills entries above diagonal (if possible) once all other entries are determined.

Once the main diagonal and the lower entries are selected, IF the matrix can be completed into a unitary matrix, the three entries above the main diagonal are uniquely determined by the unitarity of the matrix, namely since the columns (resp. the rows) are mutually orthogonal. For instance, \( y_1 \) is found by solving the below equation:

\[ \bar{x_1} \cdot y_1 = -(\bar{x_2} \cdot y_2 + \bar{x_3} \cdot y_3 ) \]

A similar equation is formed for solving \( z_2 \) and \( z_1 \).

Warning
We assume that the parameters x_1 and z_3 are zero. This should not be an issue since ESA shrinks epsilon to 0.99 if epsilon is greater than 1, but otherwise, the program is likely to throw a floating point exception.

y3_candCandidate for \( y_3 \).

Returns
A std::pair holding on the right a bool which is true iff the algorithm was able to successfully each of the remaining unitary entries. If the right is true, the left holds a std::array with all entries initialized as instances of ringZ9chi. At this point it remains to check that the given matrix is unitary and satisfies the epsilon bound.

◆ fullDiagEnumeration()

void fullDiagEnumeration ( array< vector< ringZ9 >, 3 > &  candidates,
double  theta,
double  epsilon,
int  f 
)

Unlike my previous enumeration methods, this enumerates ALL diagonal candidates for a given f, theta, and epsilon.

We take advantage of the relations found in Theorem 4.3 of Synthesis and Arithmetic of Single Qutrit Gates Going through the \( \S 4 \) replacing \( \chi^f \) with \( 3^f \), we set

\[ q = \frac{1}{4}( 2a_0 - a_3)^2 + 3a_3^2 + (2a_1 - a_4)^2 + 3a_4^2 + (2a_2 - a_5)^2 + 3a_5^2 \]

If the \( a_i \)'s represent the coefficients of a reduced element \( x \in \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9] \), then
Theorem 4.3 implies \( q(x) + q(y) + q(z) = 3^{2f} \) where \( [x,y,z]^T \) is a unit vector. Since the form \( q \) is positive definite, this implies \( q(x) \leq 3^{2f} \), and further, we may loop through all possible coefficients of \( x \).

The value of this is that, even though our desired region contains infinitely many elements of \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9] \), only finitely many of them can be entries of a unit vector.

Note
I have included a condition that prevents elements divisible by 3 from being included. Since our matrix entries will have the same \( \mbox{sde}_\chi \), we do not need to check these as these will have been checked on a previous iteraton.

candidatesA forward_list which will be filled with all candidates for a diagonal entry in a given region.
thetaThe angle of the desired two-level Givens rotator \( R_{(0,1)}^Z(\theta) \).
epsilonDesired level of precision.
fPower of three in denominator.

Computes a table of all possible values for \( x_3 \) that will also be used as a lookup table for finding all possible values of \( x_2 \) and \( y_3 \).

We use the same principles as in fullDiagEnumeration but since \( x_1 \) and \( z_3 \) are predetermined at the point this fucntion is called, we can restrict the search using the parameter M. We have that

\[ q(x_3) + q(x_1) \leq 3^{2f} \; \mbox{and} \; q(x_3) + q(z_3) \leq 3^{2f}, \]

so M (which is found by findMinQ ) is taken to be the minimum of all possible values of \( q \) ranging over candidates of \( x_1 \) and \( z_3 \). Thus, we loop over all possibilities of instance of ringZ9 whose evaluation in \( q \) is less than \( 3^{2f} - M \).

Note
I have included a condition that prevents elements divisible by 3 from being included. Since our matrix entries will have the same \( \mbox{sde}_\chi \), we do not need to check these as these will have been checked on a previous iteraton.

◆ solveSystem()

pair< ringZ9, bool > solveSystem ( ringZ9  numer,
ringZ9  denom 
)

If possible, this solves equations of the form \( ax = b \) for \( x \) in the ring \( \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_9] \).

Although the rings involved are Euclidean domains, there does not seem to be a Euclidean algorithm that is easily implementable in code to do the necessary division. However, what we can do instead is multply the numerator and denominator \( \frac{a}{b} \) by the ringZ9::partialFieldNorm of \( b \), which is \( \prod_{\sigma \in G - \{1\}} \sigma(b) \) where \( G = \mbox{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_9)) \). This makes the denominator into the field norm which is an integer, say \( N \). Now, we need only test whether the numerator times the partial field norm is divisible by \( N \), and since we may assume that this new numerator is written in the natural basis, this amounts to checking that each coefficient is divisible by \( N \).

Note
From the results of this papera, we can assume that the solution found has the same sde as that of numer and denom. Thus, our result will not require a larger value of f and we may return our answer as an instance of ringZ9.
Warning
The field norm of an element having large absolute value can have a very large norm, so there is a strong risk of integer overflow for values of \( f \) greater than two.
Parameters
numerPlays the role of \( b \) above.
denomPlays the role of \( a \) above.
Returns
A std::pair where the right is true iff the equation was solved. If the right is true, then the left returns the solution \( x \) as a ringZ9.

Since fullDiagEnumeration and fullX3Enumeration discarded all candidates with \( \mbox{sde}_\chi > 5\), (equivalent to divisibility by 3), there are only six possibilities for the sde: 0 through 5.
